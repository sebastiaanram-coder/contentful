Where content lives in an AI-native Helloprint — redesigning our content infrastructure for the next phase of growth.

Helloprint's next phase is bigger, more multi-brand, and more AI-driven than the system our content was built on was designed for. Humans and agents both need to work fluently with the same content layer — and today, neither can do it as well as they should. This document proposes a small set of design choices that, together, give us a content system that scales without rewrites every time we add a brand, a market, or a new agentic workflow — and it answers one specific question the team has raised: where the customer-facing product attribute names should live.

The one-paragraph version

Helloprint is entering a phase where both humans and AI agents need to work fluently with the same content layer — editors writing copy, JV partners shipping pages, translators localising labels, agents running GP optimisations, drafting brand-aligned content, audit­ing configurators, opening tickets. The content system we have was designed for human editors alone, and it's starting to creak under that double load. Four design choices, taken together, give us a system fit for both: (1) make a clean distinction between structured catalog data and narrative editorial content; (2) put catalog translations where the catalog data lives — in PCM, which we already own and build ourselves; (3) decouple language, brand and market so they're three independent axes instead of one overloaded locale code; (4) give JV and WLS partners scoped workspaces inside Contentful through brand tags and role-based permissions. The headline effects: a Contentful workspace that's ~10% of its current size, JV partners shipping without engineering tickets, a translation workflow built for translators, brand and market becoming configuration changes rather than projects — and a single, agent-readable content layer that lets our AI skills compose safely on top of it.

01 · Why now

Helloprint's next phase asks more of our content system than the current setup can deliver

The shape of the business is changing — in ways our content infrastructure was never designed for. Four pressures are converging at the same time.

Pressure 1

More brands, less time

Helloprint plus Allgifts, Drukwerkmax, Brandon, Quinnstheprinters and the WLS family. JV partners need real autonomy — their own pages, their own homepages, their own SEO. Today, every new brand is a multi-week engineering project.

Pressure 2

More markets, more locales

Fourteen country shops already, more to come, with brand-specific variants on top. Our locale codes now carry three different concepts (language, country, brand) in one string. Every new market is a coordinated change across Contentful, PCM, Algolia, Presta and the worker.

Pressure 3

More products, more SKUs

Contentful has crossed 400,000 entries. About 90% are structured catalog data — attribute groups and option values that no editor ever writes. The editorial team is buried under content that isn't theirs.

Pressure 4

More AI in the loop

Helloprint is moving toward an AI-native operating model — agents writing tickets, optimising configurators, drafting copy, running GP analyses, monitoring SEO. Agents reason much better over clean, single-source-of-truth data with declarative semantic tags than over a four-tier locale resolver and five sync pipelines. Our current sprawl is hostile to the agentic workflows we're betting on.

02 · The mental model

Not all content is the same. The mistake is treating it as if it is.

There are two fundamentally different shapes of content inside Helloprint. The distinction isn't about who creates it — humans write both kinds. The distinction is about the shape of the content: whether it's a structured 1:1 label tied to a catalog row, or a piece of narrative prose tied to a page.

Shape 1

Narrative content

Examples: A product page headline. A paragraph describing why our posters are great. An FAQ answer. A hero block on the homepage. A blog post. The body of a campaign landing.

How it behaves: Written for a context. One piece of copy serves a specific page, a specific moment, a specific intent. It has tone, voice, SEO purpose. Translation needs context — reading the page matters.

What it needs: Rich editor, preview, draft-publish workflow, AI writing assists. Exactly what Contentful is designed for.

Shape 2

Structured catalog labels

Examples: “A4”. “Glossy lamination”. “qty 250”. “4 days delivery”. And also: customer-friendly display names like “Thin Paper (135gsm)” that marketing writes once per option.

How it behaves: Maps 1:1 to a catalog row. Written once per option and reused everywhere. Translation is a label, not a paragraph — same term in different languages. Whether a supplier imported it or marketing wrote it doesn't change the shape.

What it needs: A structured database, a translation memory, bulk import/export, an editor optimised for "many rows, short text". Exactly what PCM is designed for.

The analogy that sticks

Contentful is the storefront. PCM is the warehouse. The storefront shows you words, photos, stories. The warehouse keeps an inventory of what we sell, how it's named, how big it is, and what it costs. The warehouse needs a label on every shelf — both the technical SKU and the customer-friendly name. Today we're stocking the storefront and labelling the warehouse from the same room. That's why the room is so crowded.

The key clarification: "human-written" doesn't equal "editorial"

It's tempting to draw the line at "humans write it" vs "machines import it". That's not the right line. Marketing humans write customer-friendly display names — one per option — and those are still structured catalog labels, not narrative content. The right line is shape: 1:1 with a catalog row goes to PCM; one-of-many in a page goes to Contentful.

03 · A concrete example — the case of "Thin Paper (135gsm)"

Where does the readable name of a paper material live?

Here's a real, everyday situation that captures the design decision in front of us. It's worth working through it carefully because it changes how we should think about which content goes where.

From our suppliers

The technical name

A supplier sends us their paper catalog. One row reads:

135gsm Machine Coated

That's the technical name. It's precise, it's standard in the print industry, and it's how the factory talks about that paper.

On our website

The customer-friendly name

Our customers don't shop for “Machine Coated” paper. They shop for something they understand. Marketing decides to call it:

Thin Paper (135gsm)

Same physical paper. Different name for a different audience.

The question

Where should “Thin Paper (135gsm)” live? It's clearly not editorial content — it's not a sentence, it has no narrative, it's not part of a page's voice. But a human writes it. Marketing decides the name. Localisation translates it into the five languages. It needs to change if the brand tone shifts.

Today, it lives in Contentful as a pcmOptionsAttribute entry, alongside hundreds of thousands of others. When marketing wants to change “Thin Paper (135gsm)” to “Lightweight (135gsm)”, they open Contentful, hunt for the right entry, change the label, and hope the Contentful Worker syncs it back through the right pipelines.

Look at what that display name actually IS

  • It maps 1:1 to a specific PCM attribute option — one customer name per supplier name.
  • It's never written from scratch — it's a marketing-friendly label for a structured catalog row.
  • It has translations (NL, FR, DE, ES, IT) just like the technical name does — same structure, same shape.
  • It belongs to the catalog, not to any particular page. The same display name appears wherever that option is shown.
  • It changes when product naming strategy changes — an event in the catalog, not in editorial content.
The answer: it belongs in PCM, right next to the supplier value

Every attribute option row in PCM gets two display fields — supplierName ("135gsm Machine Coated") and displayName ("Thin Paper (135gsm)") — plus per-locale display names for translation. Marketing edits the display name in the PCM UI. Localisation translates it there. Contentful doesn't need to know either name; it just references the option by ID and the Content Gateway brings the right display name in for the requested locale.

Why this matters beyond paper materials

The 135gsm example isn't an edge case — it's the pattern. Every attribute option in Helloprint's catalog has both a supplier-side technical reality and a customer-side readable name. Sizes, finishings, turnarounds, quantities, colour treatments, paper weights, binding types — all of them. If we get the home right for one, we get the home right for all of them. And then the catalog stops fighting against editorial work in the same workspace.

A quick mental check

If you're unsure whether a piece of content belongs in Contentful or PCM, ask one question: "If I rename this, do I rename a row in a database, or do I rewrite a paragraph?" If it's a row, it's catalog data — even if marketing wrote the new name. If it's a paragraph, it's editorial — even if the words came from a brief template.

05 · What the current setup costs us

Six concrete reasons, no jargon

If the catalog/editorial split is right, these are the things it fixes — and the things that get worse for every quarter we don't act.

Reason 1

Editors are buried under noise

A content manager searching for “FAQ delivery” or “homepage hero” in Contentful has to filter past 360,000 attribute entries that aren't pages, aren't FAQs, aren't anything they edit. The volume drowns the work.

Reason 2

Translators don't have the right tools

Contentful is a CMS. It has no translation memory, no glossary enforcement, no “don't translate this brand name” rules. So we translate “A4” over and over. We accidentally translate “Helloprint” into “Bonjour-impression”. Errors land in production. A purpose-built translation editor — the kind we can build into PCM in a few sprints — gets us all of this for the structured, repetitive catalog labels that need it most.

Reason 3

We pay for storage we don't use as content

Contentful plans are priced on entries, API calls, environments and seats. 90% of our entries are machine data that doesn't need a CMS — but we pay CMS prices for them. PCM already has this data; we're paying to host a copy.

Reason 4

Two systems quietly disagree

PCM has the canonical product. Contentful has a copy. PCM also maintains its own locale mapping (per the runbook: “PCM maintains their own mapping of Contentful locales”). Two sources of truth, kept in sync by humans and Slack messages. When they drift, customers see wrong prices or missing options.

Reason 5

JV partners can't have a clean workspace

When Allgifts opens Contentful, they see Helloprint's 400k entries. Permissions can hide some of it, but they can't make Allgifts' Contentful feel like “Allgifts' Contentful”. With the catalog gone, Allgifts editors see ~5,000 entries that are their pages and their overrides — not Helloprint's catalog spreadsheet.

Reason 6

Adding a new option is a five-step ritual

Today, adding “Soft-touch lamination” means: import to PCM → create Contentful entries (one per locale) → trigger Contentful Worker → sync to _commondata → rebuild Algolia. With PCM owning attributes, the same change is: add one row in PCM. Done.

Why this route fits Helloprint's AI-native transition

Every one of the changes above also moves us closer to a content layer agents can work with confidently. Three things specifically: (1) clear semantic boundaries — a tag like brand:allgifts is something an LLM understands natively, whereas the locale string nl-AGBE requires it to learn a Helloprint-specific convention; (2) single write paths — an agent updating a display name writes to one place (PCM) and the change propagates, rather than orchestrating writes across five systems with risk of partial failure; (3) a federated Content Gateway — one introspectable GraphQL endpoint is the natural surface for agentic skills like GP-optimization, configurator analysis, brand-voice enforcement and SEO content generation, which today have to build bespoke integrations against each pipeline. The cleaner the schema, the safer and faster the agents.

06 · Reassurance — what STAYS in Contentful

For content editors, almost nothing changes day-to-day

This is the most important thing to understand. The work that content managers, copywriters, marketers and JV editors do every day — that all stays in Contentful, with a faster, cleaner workspace. The change is invisible to most of the team.

Stays in Contentful (your day-to-day)

  • Product page narrative — H1, description, USPs, CTAs
  • Homepages, category pages, landing pages
  • Blog posts and editorial articles
  • FAQs and help content
  • Hero blocks, rich-text blocks, media blocks
  • Navigation, header, footer, mega-menus
  • SEO meta titles & descriptions
  • Campaign & seasonal content
  • Marketing photography & imagery
  • Email templates & transactional copy
  • Brand-specific overrides per JV / WLS
  • The configurator recipe (defaults, recommend badges)

Moves to PCM (the catalog brain)

  • Product master data (SKUs, codes)
  • Attribute group definitions ("Paper material", "Size")
  • Option values, both names: supplierName ("135gsm Machine Coated") and displayName ("Thin Paper (135gsm)")
  • Pricing tables and quantity breaks
  • Per-locale translations of displayName (NL/FR/DE/ES/IT)
  • Product imagery (catalog/spec shots)
  • Technical specifications
  • Delivery and turnaround data

All of this is 1:1 with a catalog row. Whether a supplier sent it or marketing wrote it, the shape is the same — structured, repeated, tied to an option.

07 · Translations — the biggest question, answered head-on

Translations don't disappear. They live where the data lives — inside PCM.

This is the fear we hear most: “If product attributes leave Contentful, how do we translate them?” The honest answer is that we build a small, focused translation editor into PCM itself. We already own and build PCM — adding a translation feature to it is faster, cheaper and more controllable than introducing a third-party tool.

Today

Translations in Contentful

  • No translation memory — "A4" gets translated 100+ times
  • No glossary — brand terms get accidentally translated
  • No "do not translate" rules
  • No machine-translation pre-fill
  • Translators see the labels but not the customer-facing context
  • Drift between Contentful and PCM's own locale mapping — two sources of truth
Tomorrow

Translations natively in PCM

  • Translation memory built-in — "A4" translated once, reused everywhere
  • Glossary table — "Helloprint", "DTP", brand names protected
  • Optional machine-translation pre-fill (DeepL / Google) for first drafts
  • Translator UI grouped by attribute — "all paper materials at once"
  • Bulk CSV import/export for translators who prefer offline work
  • One system · one login · no vendor dependency
Why PCM-native is the right call (and not a TMS like CrowdIn)

Catalog labels are short (1–5 words), highly repetitive ("A4", "Glossy"), and translated by internal Helloprint staff who already know the catalog. A full-fat third-party TMS is built for a different problem — long-form content translated at distance by external agencies. For our use case, the few features that matter (TM, glossary, bulk edit) cost a handful of sprints to build natively in PCM — and we avoid an extra vendor, a per-word bill, sync plumbing, and a second tool for translators to learn.

Bonus: translations stay visible inside Contentful

For editors who want context, we install a Contentful Sidebar App that reads the live PCM translations. When you open a product page in Contentful, the sidebar shows the option labels for the current locale, with a one-click link to open that row in PCM's translation editor. You see everything; you can only edit the right thing in the right place.

What about the B2B Portal & CrowdIn?

This proposal is about the catalog path only. If the Business Portal / RMH is already using CrowdIn for UI strings and that's working well, leave it untouched — it's a separate decision and a separate surface. We're not consolidating tools for the sake of it; we're putting catalog translations where the catalog data lives.

10 · What's hard about this — honestly

Three real tradeoffs

This change isn't free. Here's where we'd need investment and what would feel uncomfortable.

PCM needs to build a translation editor

PCM has product data and a Contentful locale mapping, but doesn't yet have a first-class translation editor with TM, glossary and bulk operations. This is the largest piece of engineering work in the proposal — roughly a quarter of one engineer's time, depending on what's already there. The upside: we own it forever and can shape it to fit how our translators actually work.

Algolia indexer rewires

Today the indexer pulls from Contentful via the worker and the GitLab _commondata repo. Tomorrow it pulls product data and translations from PCM, and page data from Contentful. The plumbing is straightforward but it's the most-touched pipeline. Expect a few sprints.

Translators switch tools for catalog work

Today they translate catalog labels inside Contentful. Tomorrow they translate them inside PCM. Same kind of work, different home — and the new home has TM, glossary and bulk edit. Page copy still lives in Contentful, so editorial translators stay where they are. The Sidebar App means you can still see the PCM translations from inside Contentful when you need context.

11 · FAQ — the objections we'll hear

Anticipated questions

"But we like the Contentful UI for editing translations. Why give that up?"

You keep it for the content that benefits from it: page copy, FAQs, hero blocks, narrative descriptions. That's all the rich-text and contextual work where the CF editor shines.

For option labels, the Contentful UI is actually quite poor — one entry at a time, no TM, no glossary, no batch view. PCM's new translation editor is built specifically for this kind of structured, repetitive content. Same translator, better tool for the job.

"What if PCM goes down? Does the website break?"

Same as today, but slightly safer. Today, if PCM goes down, new products and price updates stop. With the new model, the Content Gateway caches the last known good product data at the edge, so the site keeps rendering existing products with the most recent attributes. PCM being source-of-truth doesn't mean PCM has to be hit on every page request.

"Will JV partners (Allgifts, Drukwerkmax, Quinnstheprinters) lose their flexibility?"

The opposite — they gain it. Today they share a workspace with 400k entries that aren't theirs. Tomorrow they get a clean scoped workspace, can create full pages, override option labels for their brand, and ship without an engineering ticket. The JV brand becomes a first-class concept in Contentful (a brand tag), not a locale suffix.

"WLS partners need homepage / about / legal pages. Does this still work?"

Yes. WLS partners get a more constrained role — they can edit their homepage, about and legal pages, all tagged with their brand. They can't touch the catalog (which is in PCM anyway) or Helloprint's editorial content. Exactly the model you described.

"What about all the historic translations we've built up in Contentful for option labels?"

We migrate them. The first import into PCM's translation table uses our existing Contentful translations as the seed, and the TM is populated from that same export. Nothing is lost — everything we've translated becomes the foundation that makes future translations faster. This is a one-time migration script, run once during cutover.

"How long until we see the benefit?"

Editorial benefit (cleaner Contentful for editors) appears the moment the catalog data leaves — that can be a single migration sprint. Translation benefit needs PCM's new translation editor to be built — bigger, probably a quarter for a solid first version. JV-partner benefit appears when the brand tag and role-based permissions are in place — can be done independently in parallel.

"Why now? It's been like this for years."

Three pressures converging. (1) JV partners need real flexibility we can't give them today. (2) Contentful's bill grows linearly with entries. (3) The translation pipeline has documented drift bugs that no amount of patching fixes — the root cause is that translation is happening in the wrong tool. Each of those alone might be tolerable; together they make this the right moment.

"Who owns this if we say yes?"

Three owners. PCM team owns the catalog data and the new translation editor. Content engineering owns the new content model and the Content Gateway. Localisation owns the translator workflow inside PCM — what the editor looks like, how reviews work, how bulk operations behave. One product owner across all three to keep them honest about sequencing.

"How does this fit our AI-native ambitions?"

Directly. Every Helloprint agent we already run — GP-optimization, configurator audits, brand-voice enforcement, SEO content generation, brief-to-ticket, keyword analysis — relies on a clean read of what content exists and a safe write path to change it. Today both are hard. After the restructure, an agent gets a single GraphQL endpoint that exposes catalog data, page content, brand and market tags as first-class concepts. Writes go to one source per concept. That's not a side effect — it's the foundation on which we can compose the next ten skills without each one having to learn the entire sprawl.

Concretely: the GP-optimization skill's "Mode 4" (read configurator state from Contentful, write recommendations back) only works at scale once the content model is mature. The same is true for the brand-voice enforcement and content-generation skills — they need to know which entry is which brand, which market, which language, without parsing locale suffixes. The restructure is what makes those skills production-grade rather than per-product spikes.

12 · Next steps

What we're asking the team to agree to today

Three small decisions. Each one is reversible. Together they unlock the whole change.

Decision 1 · The principle

Agree, in principle, that product attributes belong in PCM and editorial content belongs in Contentful. If we agree on the principle, we can plan the “how” together.

Decision 2 · The PCM scoping

Authorise a one-week spike with the PCM team to scope what the translation editor needs to look like — locale model, TM lookup, glossary, bulk operations, API contract. Nothing built yet; just a clear picture of the work.

Decision 3 · The pilot product

Pick one product (suggested: Posters) to pilot the new model end-to-end — catalog and translations in PCM, copy in Contentful. If it works for Posters, it works for the rest.

If we agree, the order of operations is…

Week 1: PCM translation-editor scoping spike. Week 2–3: Posters pilot in a Contentful sandbox env, with translations served from a thin PCM translation API. Week 4: Demo to the whole team with a real PDP composed from PCM + Contentful. From there, we plan the full migration with confidence and a working reference implementation.
