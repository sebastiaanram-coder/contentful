Where content lives in an AI-native Helloprint — redesigning our content infrastructure for the next phase of growth.

Helloprint's next phase is bigger, more multi-brand, and more AI-driven than the system our content was built on was designed for. Humans and agents both need to work fluently with the same content layer — and today, neither can do it as well as they should. This document proposes a small set of design choices that, together, give us a content system that scales without rewrites every time we add a brand, a market, or a new agentic workflow — and it answers one specific question the team has raised: where the customer-facing product attribute names should live.

The one-paragraph version

Helloprint is entering a phase where both humans and AI agents need to work fluently with the same content layer — editors writing copy, JV partners shipping pages, translators localising labels, agents running GP optimisations, drafting brand-aligned content, audit­ing configurators, opening tickets. The content system we have was designed for human editors alone, and it's starting to creak under that double load. Four design choices, taken together, give us a system fit for both: (1) make a clean distinction between structured catalog data and narrative Commercial Content; (2) put catalog translations where the catalog data lives — in the Platform Catalog, which we already own and build ourselves; (3) decouple language, brand and market so they're three independent axes instead of one overloaded locale code; (4) give JV and WLS partners scoped workspaces inside Contentful through brand tags and role-based permissions. The headline effects: a Contentful workspace that's ~10% of its current size, JV partners shipping without engineering tickets, a translation workflow built for translators, brand and market becoming configuration changes rather than projects — and a single, agent-readable content layer that lets our AI skills compose safely on top of it.

Terminology

The vocabulary we use, and how it maps

This proposal uses the terminology established in the Helloprint Platform Product Data & Content Setup memo (v1.0). The terms below are the canonical ones; familiarity with them makes the rest of this document precise rather than approximate.

Helloprint Platform

The organisation that purchases products from suppliers, manages the supply chain, and sells to merchants via Transfer Pricing. Operates the Platform Catalog, Platform Catalog API and Partners Hub. Not the same as Helloprint Ecommerce.

Helloprint Ecommerce

The consumer-facing storefront of Helloprint. Architecturally one merchant of the Platform — alongside Allgifts, Bizay and others. Sources products via the Platform Catalog API like any other merchant.

Platform Catalog

The master catalog of products maintained by Helloprint Platform. Contains Catalog Products, their SKUs and associated Factual Content. Structured by country of delivery. The single source of truth for factual product data.

Merchant Catalog

A specific merchant's product catalog, sourced from the Platform Catalog via the API. Can be a curated subset. Also structured by country of delivery. Inherits factual content from the Platform Catalog.

Catalog Product

A template type within the Platform Catalog — the canonical product definition at the category level (e.g. "Business Card", "Flyer", "T-shirt"). Contains one or more SKUs.

SKU

A specific configuration of a Catalog Product, defined by quantity + delivery time. "250 pcs / 5 days" and "500 pcs / 8 days" are two SKUs of the same Catalog Product. Transfer Pricing is applied at SKU level.

Commercial Product

How a merchant presents 1+ SKUs on their storefront. A Commercial Product always maps to exactly one Catalog Product. One Catalog Product can become multiple Commercial Products (e.g. "Standard" and "Premium" variants of the same template). This is what a CF entry represents in our model.

Platform Catalog API

The programmatic interface through which merchants access the Platform Catalog — Catalog Products, SKUs, Factual Content and pricing. All merchants use this API; there is no direct database access. The Content Gateway in this proposal sits on top of it.

Factual Content

All factual content describing a Catalog Product — specifications, dimensions, materials, production options, print specs, artwork requirements. Lives in the Platform Catalog. Cannot be overridden per merchant at the Platform level.

Commercial Content

Marketing and storefront content used to present a Commercial Product to end customers — product names, descriptions, imagery, CTAs. Owned and managed by the merchant in their CMS (Contentful for Helloprint Ecommerce and Allgifts today).

Transfer Pricing

The price agreements between a merchant and Helloprint Platform. Three components: product pricing (per SKU), shipping pricing (carrier and destination-based), and a services fee for supply-chain management. Applied at SKU level. Each merchant has its own.

Suppliers & Partners Hub

Print producers configure their products and pricing via Partners Hub. Their input is the raw data the Helloprint Platform team cleans, enriches and exposes through the Platform Catalog. Suppliers are not merchants.

The hierarchy at a glance

Catalog Product (template, owned by Platform) → contains many SKUs (configurations, also Platform) → presented to customers as Commercial Products (owned by merchant, content in their CMS). A single Catalog Product can become one or more Commercial Products per merchant.

01 · Why now

Helloprint's next phase asks more of our content system than the current setup can deliver

The shape of the business is changing — in ways our content infrastructure was never designed for. Four pressures are converging at the same time.

Pressure 1

More brands, less time

Helloprint plus Allgifts, Drukwerkmax, Brandon, Quinnstheprinters and the WLS family. JV partners need real autonomy — their own pages, their own homepages, their own SEO. Today, every new brand is a multi-week engineering project.

Pressure 2

More markets, more locales

Fourteen country shops already, more to come, with brand-specific variants on top. Our locale codes now carry three different concepts (language, country, brand) in one string. Every new market is a coordinated change across Contentful, the Platform Catalog, Algolia, Presta and the worker.

Pressure 3

More products, more SKUs

Contentful has crossed 400,000 entries. About 90% are structured catalog data — attribute groups and option values that no editor ever writes. The editorial team is buried under content that isn't theirs.

Pressure 4

More AI in the loop

Helloprint is moving toward an AI-native operating model — agents writing tickets, optimising configurators, drafting copy, running GP analyses, monitoring SEO. Agents reason much better over clean, single-source-of-truth data with declarative semantic tags than over a four-tier locale resolver and five sync pipelines. Our current sprawl is hostile to the agentic workflows we're betting on.

02 · The mental model

Not all content is the same. The mistake is treating it as if it is.

Per the Helloprint Platform memo, there are two distinct kinds of content: Factual Content (specs, dimensions, materials, all the structured catalog data) and Commercial Content (the narrative, marketing-driven content that presents a product to a customer). The distinction isn't about who creates it — humans write both kinds. The distinction is about the shape and ownership: Factual lives in the Platform Catalog and is shared across all merchants; Commercial lives in each merchant's CMS and is theirs alone.

Commercial Content

Narrative, per merchant

Examples: The headline on a product page. A paragraph describing why these posters are great. An FAQ answer. A hero block on the homepage. A blog post.

How it behaves: Written for a context. One piece of copy serves a specific page, a specific moment, a specific intent. It has tone, voice, SEO purpose. Each merchant writes their own.

Where it lives: In the merchant's CMS — Contentful for Helloprint Ecommerce and Allgifts today.

Factual Content

Structured, shared

Examples: “A4”. “Glossy lamination”. “qty 250”. “4 days delivery”. Print specs, artwork requirements (bleed, DPI, format). And also: customer-friendly display names like “Thin Paper (135gsm)”.

How it behaves: Maps 1:1 to a catalog row. Written once per option and reused by every merchant. Cannot be overridden per merchant at the Platform level.

Where it lives: In the Platform Catalog, maintained by the Helloprint Platform team.

The hierarchy underneath

Both kinds of content sit on a structural hierarchy from the memo. Worth holding in your head when reading the rest of this document:

FIG. 0 — The hierarchy that underpins everything. Catalog Product and SKU are Platform-owned; Commercial Product is merchant-owned. What this proposal calls "a CF entry" is, in memo vocabulary, a Commercial Product.
The analogy that sticks

Contentful is the storefront. The Platform Catalog is the warehouse. The storefront shows you words, photos, stories — one per merchant. The warehouse keeps an inventory of what we sell, how it's named, how big it is, and what it costs — shared across all merchants. The warehouse needs a label on every shelf, both the technical SKU code and the customer-friendly name. Today we're stocking the storefront and labelling the warehouse from the same room. That's why the room is so crowded.

The key clarification: "human-written" doesn't equal "Commercial"

It's tempting to draw the line at "humans write it" vs "machines import it". That's not the right line. Marketing humans write customer-friendly display names — one per option — and those are still Factual Content (structured catalog labels), not Commercial Content. The right line is shape: 1:1 with a catalog row goes to the Platform Catalog; one-of-many on a page goes to Contentful.

03 · A concrete example — the case of "Thin Paper (135gsm)"

Where does the readable name of a paper material live?

Here's a real, everyday situation that captures the design decision in front of us. It's worth working through it carefully because it changes how we should think about which content goes where.

From our suppliers

The technical name

A supplier sends us their paper catalog. One row reads:

135gsm Machine Coated

That's the technical name. It's precise, it's standard in the print industry, and it's how the factory talks about that paper.

On our website

The customer-friendly name

Our customers don't shop for “Machine Coated” paper. They shop for something they understand. Marketing decides to call it:

Thin Paper (135gsm)

Same physical paper. Different name for a different audience.

The question

Where should “Thin Paper (135gsm)” live? It's clearly not Commercial Content — it's not a sentence, it has no narrative, it's not part of a page's voice. But a human writes it. Marketing decides the name. Localisation translates it into the five languages. It needs to change if the brand tone shifts.

Today, it lives in Contentful as a pcmOptionsAttribute entry, alongside hundreds of thousands of others. When marketing wants to change “Thin Paper (135gsm)” to “Lightweight (135gsm)”, they open Contentful, hunt for the right entry, change the label, and hope the Contentful Worker syncs it back through the right pipelines.

Look at what that display name actually IS

  • It maps 1:1 to a specific Platform Catalog attribute option — one customer name per supplier name.
  • It's never written from scratch — it's a marketing-friendly label for a structured catalog row.
  • It has translations (NL, FR, DE, ES, IT) just like the technical name does — same structure, same shape.
  • It belongs to the catalog, not to any particular page. The same display name appears wherever that option is shown.
  • It changes when product naming strategy changes — an event in the catalog, not in Commercial Content.
The answer: it belongs in the Platform Catalog, right next to the supplier value

Every attribute option row in the Platform Catalog gets two display fields — supplierName ("135gsm Machine Coated") and displayName ("Thin Paper (135gsm)") — plus per-locale display names for translation. Marketing edits the display name in the Platform Catalog UI. Localisation translates it there. Contentful doesn't need to know either name; it just references the option by ID and the Content Gateway brings the right display name in for the requested locale.

Why this matters beyond paper materials

The 135gsm example isn't an edge case — it's the pattern. Every attribute option in Helloprint's catalog has both a supplier-side technical reality and a customer-side readable name. Sizes, finishings, turnarounds, quantities, colour treatments, paper weights, binding types — all of them. If we get the home right for one, we get the home right for all of them. And then the catalog stops fighting against editorial work in the same workspace.

A quick mental check

If you're unsure whether a piece of content belongs in Contentful or the Platform Catalog, ask one question: "If I rename this, do I rename a row in a database, or do I rewrite a paragraph?" If it's a row, it's catalog data — even if marketing wrote the new name. If it's a paragraph, it's editorial — even if the words came from a brief template.

05 · What the current setup costs us

Six concrete reasons, no jargon

If the catalog/editorial split is right, these are the things it fixes — and the things that get worse for every quarter we don't act.

Reason 1

Editors are buried under noise

A content manager searching for “FAQ delivery” or “homepage hero” in Contentful has to filter past 360,000 attribute entries that aren't pages, aren't FAQs, aren't anything they edit. The volume drowns the work.

Reason 2

Translators don't have the right tools

Contentful is a CMS. It has no translation memory, no glossary enforcement, no “don't translate this brand name” rules. So we translate “A4” over and over. We accidentally translate “Helloprint” into “Bonjour-impression”. Errors land in production. A purpose-built translation editor — the kind we can build into the Platform Catalog in a few sprints — gets us all of this for the structured, repetitive catalog labels that need it most.

Reason 3

We pay for storage we don't use as content

Contentful plans are priced on entries, API calls, environments and seats. 90% of our entries are machine data that doesn't need a CMS — but we pay CMS prices for them. The Platform Catalog already has this data; we're paying to host a copy.

Reason 4

Two systems quietly disagree

The Platform Catalog has the canonical product. Contentful has a copy. The catalog system also maintains its own locale mapping (per the runbook: the catalog "maintains their own mapping of Contentful locales"). Two sources of truth, kept in sync by humans and Slack messages. When they drift, customers see wrong prices or missing options.

Reason 5

JV partners can't have a clean workspace

When Allgifts opens Contentful, they see Helloprint's 400k entries. Permissions can hide some of it, but they can't make Allgifts' Contentful feel like “Allgifts' Contentful”. With the catalog gone, Allgifts editors see ~5,000 entries that are their pages and their overrides — not Helloprint's catalog spreadsheet.

Reason 6

Adding a new option is a five-step ritual

Today, adding “Soft-touch lamination” means: import to the Platform Catalog → create Contentful entries (one per locale) → trigger Contentful Worker → sync to _commondata → rebuild Algolia. With the Platform Catalog owning attributes, the same change is: add one row in the Platform Catalog. Done.

Why this route fits Helloprint's AI-native transition

Every one of the changes above also moves us closer to a content layer agents can work with confidently. Three things specifically: (1) clear semantic boundaries — a tag like brand:allgifts is something an LLM understands natively, whereas the locale string nl-AGBE requires it to learn a Helloprint-specific convention; (2) single write paths — an agent updating a display name writes to one place (Platform Catalog) and the change propagates, rather than orchestrating writes across five systems with risk of partial failure; (3) a federated Content Gateway — one introspectable GraphQL endpoint is the natural surface for agentic skills like GP-optimization, configurator analysis, brand-voice enforcement and SEO content generation, which today have to build bespoke integrations against each pipeline. The cleaner the schema, the safer and faster the agents.

06 · Reassurance — what STAYS in Contentful

For content editors, almost nothing changes day-to-day

This is the most important thing to understand. The work that content managers, copywriters, marketers and JV editors do every day — that all stays in Contentful, with a faster, cleaner workspace. The change is invisible to most of the team.

Stays in Contentful (your day-to-day)

  • Product page narrative — H1, description, USPs, CTAs
  • Homepages, category pages, landing pages
  • Blog posts and editorial articles
  • FAQs and help content
  • Hero blocks, rich-text blocks, media blocks
  • Navigation, header, footer, mega-menus
  • SEO meta titles & descriptions
  • Campaign & seasonal content
  • Marketing photography & imagery
  • Email templates & transactional copy
  • Brand-specific overrides per JV / WLS
  • The configurator recipe (defaults, recommend badges)

Moves to the Platform Catalog (the catalog brain)

  • Product master data (SKUs, codes)
  • Attribute group definitions ("Paper material", "Size")
  • Option values, both names: supplierName ("135gsm Machine Coated") and displayName ("Thin Paper (135gsm)")
  • Pricing tables and quantity breaks
  • Per-locale translations of displayName (NL/FR/DE/ES/IT)
  • Product imagery (catalog/spec shots)
  • Technical specifications
  • Delivery and turnaround data

All of this is 1:1 with a catalog row. Whether a supplier sent it or marketing wrote it, the shape is the same — structured, repeated, tied to an option.

07 · Translations — the biggest question, answered head-on

Translations don't disappear. They live where the data lives — inside the Platform Catalog.

This is the fear we hear most: “If product attributes leave Contentful, how do we translate them?” The honest answer is that we build a small, focused translation editor into the Platform Catalog itself. We already own and build Platform Catalog — adding a translation feature to it is faster, cheaper and more controllable than introducing a third-party tool.

Today

Translations in Contentful

  • No translation memory — "A4" gets translated 100+ times
  • No glossary — brand terms get accidentally translated
  • No "do not translate" rules
  • No machine-translation pre-fill
  • Translators see the labels but not the customer-facing context
  • Drift between Contentful and Platform Catalog's own locale mapping — two sources of truth
Tomorrow

Translations natively in the Platform Catalog

  • Translation memory built-in — "A4" translated once, reused everywhere
  • Glossary table — "Helloprint", "DTP", brand names protected
  • Optional machine-translation pre-fill (DeepL / Google) for first drafts
  • Translator UI grouped by attribute — "all paper materials at once"
  • Bulk CSV import/export for translators who prefer offline work
  • One system · one login · no vendor dependency
Why Platform Catalog-native is the right call (and not a TMS like CrowdIn)

Catalog labels are short (1–5 words), highly repetitive ("A4", "Glossy"), and translated by internal Helloprint staff who already know the catalog. A full-fat third-party TMS is built for a different problem — long-form content translated at distance by external agencies. For our use case, the few features that matter (TM, glossary, bulk edit) cost a handful of sprints to build natively in the Platform Catalog — and we avoid an extra vendor, a per-word bill, sync plumbing, and a second tool for translators to learn.

Bonus: translations stay visible inside Contentful

For editors who want context, we install a Contentful Sidebar App that reads the live Platform Catalog translations. When you open a product page in Contentful, the sidebar shows the option labels for the current locale, with a one-click link to open that row in the Platform Catalog's translation editor. You see everything; you can only edit the right thing in the right place.

What about the B2B Portal & CrowdIn?

This proposal is about the catalog path only. If the Business Portal / RMH is already using CrowdIn for UI strings and that's working well, leave it untouched — it's a separate decision and a separate surface. We're not consolidating tools for the sake of it; we're putting catalog translations where the catalog data lives.

10 · What's hard about this — honestly

Three real tradeoffs

This change isn't free. Here's where we'd need investment and what would feel uncomfortable.

Platform Catalog needs to build a translation editor

Platform Catalog has product data and a Contentful locale mapping, but doesn't yet have a first-class translation editor with TM, glossary and bulk operations. This is the largest piece of engineering work in the proposal — roughly a quarter of one engineer's time, depending on what's already there. The upside: we own it forever and can shape it to fit how our translators actually work.

Algolia indexer rewires

Today the indexer pulls from Contentful via the worker and the GitLab _commondata repo. Tomorrow it pulls product data and translations from the Platform Catalog, and page data from Contentful. The plumbing is straightforward but it's the most-touched pipeline. Expect a few sprints.

Translators switch tools for catalog work

Today they translate catalog labels inside Contentful. Tomorrow they translate them inside the Platform Catalog. Same kind of work, different home — and the new home has TM, glossary and bulk edit. Page copy still lives in Contentful, so editorial translators stay where they are. The Sidebar App means you can still see the Platform Catalog translations from inside Contentful when you need context.

11 · FAQ — the objections we'll hear

Anticipated questions

"But we like the Contentful UI for editing translations. Why give that up?"

You keep it for the content that benefits from it: page copy, FAQs, hero blocks, narrative descriptions. That's all the rich-text and contextual work where the CF editor shines.

For option labels, the Contentful UI is actually quite poor — one entry at a time, no TM, no glossary, no batch view. Platform Catalog's new translation editor is built specifically for this kind of structured, repetitive content. Same translator, better tool for the job.

"What if Platform Catalog goes down? Does the website break?"

Same as today, but slightly safer. Today, if the Platform Catalog goes down, new products and price updates stop. With the new model, the Content Gateway caches the last known good product data at the edge, so the site keeps rendering existing products with the most recent attributes. The Platform Catalog being source-of-truth doesn't mean it has to be hit on every page request.

"Will JV partners (Allgifts, Drukwerkmax, Quinnstheprinters) lose their flexibility?"

The opposite — they gain it. Today they share a workspace with 400k entries that aren't theirs. Tomorrow they get a clean scoped workspace, can create full pages, override option labels for their brand, and ship without an engineering ticket. The JV brand becomes a first-class concept in Contentful (a brand tag), not a locale suffix.

"WLS partners need homepage / about / legal pages. Does this still work?"

Yes. WLS partners get a more constrained role — they can edit their homepage, about and legal pages, all tagged with their brand. They can't touch the catalog (which is in the Platform Catalog anyway) or Helloprint's Commercial Content. Exactly the model you described.

"What about all the historic translations we've built up in Contentful for option labels?"

We migrate them. The first import into the Platform Catalog's translation table uses our existing Contentful translations as the seed, and the TM is populated from that same export. Nothing is lost — everything we've translated becomes the foundation that makes future translations faster. This is a one-time migration script, run once during cutover.

"How long until we see the benefit?"

Editorial benefit (cleaner Contentful for editors) appears the moment the catalog data leaves — that can be a single migration sprint. Translation benefit needs Platform Catalog's new translation editor to be built — bigger, probably a quarter for a solid first version. JV-partner benefit appears when the brand tag and role-based permissions are in place — can be done independently in parallel.

"Why now? It's been like this for years."

Three pressures converging. (1) JV partners need real flexibility we can't give them today. (2) Contentful's bill grows linearly with entries. (3) The translation pipeline has documented drift bugs that no amount of patching fixes — the root cause is that translation is happening in the wrong tool. Each of those alone might be tolerable; together they make this the right moment.

"Who owns this if we say yes?"

Three owners. The Catalog team owns the Platform Catalog data and the new translation editor. Content engineering owns the new content model and the Content Gateway. Localisation owns the translator workflow inside the Platform Catalog — what the editor looks like, how reviews work, how bulk operations behave. One product owner across all three to keep them honest about sequencing.

"How does this fit our AI-native ambitions?"

Directly. Every Helloprint agent we already run — GP-optimization, configurator audits, brand-voice enforcement, SEO content generation, brief-to-ticket, keyword analysis — relies on a clean read of what content exists and a safe write path to change it. Today both are hard. After the restructure, an agent gets a single GraphQL endpoint that exposes catalog data, page content, brand and market tags as first-class concepts. Writes go to one source per concept. That's not a side effect — it's the foundation on which we can compose the next ten skills without each one having to learn the entire sprawl.

Concretely: the GP-optimization skill's "Mode 4" (read configurator state from Contentful, write recommendations back) only works at scale once the content model is mature. The same is true for the brand-voice enforcement and content-generation skills — they need to know which entry is which brand, which market, which language, without parsing locale suffixes. The restructure is what makes those skills production-grade rather than per-product spikes.

12 · Next steps

What we're asking the team to agree to today

Three small decisions. Each one is reversible. Together they unlock the whole change.

Decision 1 · The principle

Agree, in principle, that product attributes belong in the Platform Catalog and Commercial Content belongs in Contentful. If we agree on the principle, we can plan the “how” together.

Decision 2 · The Platform Catalog scoping

Authorise a one-week spike with the Catalog team to scope what the Platform Catalog translation editor needs to look like — locale model, TM lookup, glossary, bulk operations, API contract. Nothing built yet; just a clear picture of the work.

Decision 3 · The pilot product

Pick one product (suggested: Posters) to pilot the new model end-to-end — catalog and translations in the Platform Catalog, copy in Contentful. If it works for Posters, it works for the rest.

If we agree, the order of operations is…

Product implementation matrix

Field-by-field, for the 47 fields in today's product content type — where each one lives in the new architecture across three systems: Contentful, the Platform Catalog, and the Merchant Catalog.

The model

The three systems we're modelling against

Before placing fields, let's name the systems precisely. What we have is a multi-tenant catalog architecture with two distinct catalog tiers, plus a per-merchant Commercial Content layer.

Tier 1 · shared

Platform Catalog

What it is: Helloprint's master catalog. Every product that could exist in any merchant's shop lives here.

What it owns: Catalog facts that are the same regardless of who's selling — product master data, attribute structure, technical specs, submission rules, configurator schema, catalog imagery.

Who writes to it: Helloprint's catalog team. One source for all merchants.

Tier 2 · per merchant

Merchant Catalog

What it is: Each merchant's curated selection from the Platform Catalog. Helloprint is a merchant. Allgifts, Drukwerkmax, Quinnstheprinters and every WLS shop are merchants too.

What it owns: Which Catalog Products this merchant has activated, availability per country of delivery, the merchant's Transfer Pricing arrangement (product per SKU + shipping + services fee), promos, search and filter rules.

Who writes to it: The merchant. Activating a product here triggers a Contentful entry for it.

Tier 3 · per merchant

Contentful

What it is: Commercial Content layer for one merchant's storefront. Each merchant has their own CF entries for the products they've activated in their Merchant Catalog.

What it owns: Page narrative, marketing copy, SEO, brand-specific overrides on default catalog values.

Who writes to it: That merchant's content team.

The cleanest way to think about it

Activating a product in a Merchant Catalog creates a corresponding entry in that merchant's Contentful. The CF entry inherits defaults from the Platform Catalog — product name, default imagery, technical specs — and the merchant can override any of them per locale, market or campaign. Deactivating in the Merchant Catalog archives the CF entry. One activation event, one editorial workspace.

Migration plan

Four phases, no big-bang cutover

We don't migrate inside the current Contentful space — we stand up new clean CF entries per merchant alongside it, build out the Platform/Merchant Catalog separation, run both architectures in parallel, and only retire the old space once every frontend has moved across.

Phase 0 · Today

One bloated space

Current Contentful space holds all 47 product fields, ~400k entries total, mixed catalog + editorial. The catalog tier exists but is mirrored into Contentful. Frontends read via 5 pipelines.

Phase 1 · Build in parallel

New clean CF + catalog split

Stand up a new CF space with the 15 editorial fields per merchant + the 2 override fields. Formalise the Platform Catalog (master) vs Merchant Catalog (per-merchant) split. Old space untouched.

Phase 2 · Bridge

Content Gateway up

Build the Content Gateway that composes new CF + Platform Catalog + Merchant Catalog into one GraphQL endpoint. Migrate frontends one by one to read from the gateway.

Phase 3 · Sunset

Old space deprecated

Once every frontend (storefronts, blog, B2B portal, Algolia indexer) reads exclusively from the gateway, set the old space to read-only, then archive it.

✓
Why this works without breaking anything

The frontends don't migrate from "old CF" to "new CF". They migrate from "old CF + 5 custom pipelines" to "one GraphQL gateway". From the frontend's perspective, the change is replacing many noisy reads with one clean read. The old Contentful space stops being read from naturally, not by force, as each frontend cuts over. When the last one moves, the old space can be deprecated with confidence because nothing depends on it anymore.

Open questions

Four fields without a decision yet

These are not catalog-vs-editorial debates — they're "do we still need this field?" or "where does it logically belong?" questions. Surface them to the team before the matrix is finalised.

?Meta Robot Content short

Per-page indexing directive (e.g. noindex,follow).

The question: Should this be in Contentful at all? Most products use the same value across all locales. Could be a Platform Catalog default with a CF override only when a merchant specifically needs noindex for a particular page. Or could leave Contentful entirely and live in a routing / SEO config layer.

?Search Name short

Algolia-friendly product name override.

The question: Do we still need this? With the new model, Title already covers public name (with the Platform default + CF override pattern). Search Name may be redundant. Recommend deprecating unless a concrete use case surfaces.

?Search Tags list

Algolia keywords list.

The question: Stays in Contentful for now, but possibly belongs elsewhere — Platform Catalog (if keywords are catalog-wide) or Merchant Catalog (if each merchant tunes their own keywords). Worth deciding before migration.

?Search settings bool

Search visibility toggle.

The question: Same as Search Tags — could stay in CF, but might fit better in Merchant Catalog as a per-merchant search behaviour setting alongside Search boost. Pick one home.

Spotlight

How the "Both" pattern works — Title and Product Images

Two fields share this pattern. Both have a sensible default coming from the Platform Catalog, and both can be overridden by a merchant in their own Contentful when they want their own voice or visuals.

FIG. M-1 — The Both pattern. Platform Catalog provides defaults that always exist; Merchant CF holds optional overrides; the Gateway picks the most specific available value, per locale and per market.

Why both, not one

Helloprint sells the same physical "Folding Box" as Allgifts and Drukwerkmax. The product is identical. But each merchant might want a different headline or different lifestyle photography to match their brand. That's the override use case: same SKU, different presentation.

The override is the exception

Most products in most merchants won't override anything — they'll just use the Platform defaults. The override only exists where a merchant cares enough to provide their own value. This keeps Merchant CF spaces lean and avoids forcing every merchant to manage values they don't have a strong opinion on.

The shape of a product in the new world

A merchant's CF entry for a product has 15 fields that are all editorial decisions worth a human's attention — plus 2 optional override fields (Title, Product Images) for when the merchant wants to deviate from the Platform default. Everything else is read from the Platform Catalog or Merchant Catalog at runtime by the Content Gateway. No machine-written blobs, no hidden legacy fields, no orphan catalog config.

Implementation · spaces, environments, roles · v1.0 Draft

One shared space,
scoped by merchant tags.

How Marie at Helloprint Ecommerce, Lisa at Allgifts, and Pieter at Drukwerkmax can all work in the same Contentful workspace without ever stepping on each other's content — and how a JV partner gets their own clean editorial workspace without us provisioning a new CF space per brand.

The decision

Why one shared space, not one per merchant

Two viable models. We recommend the simpler one with a clear escape hatch when isolation becomes a hard requirement.

Recommended

One shared CF space

All merchants work in the same space. Their content is separated by merchant:* tags and editors are scoped by role policies that filter on those tags.

  • One subscription tier — cost doesn't multiply by merchant count
  • One content model to maintain — add a field once, every merchant benefits
  • One set of integrations (Gateway, Algolia, cache) — they filter by tag
  • Onboarding a new merchant is a tag + a role — ~5 minutes of admin work
  • JVs still see a clean workspace via role policies, not via separate spaces
Escape hatch

Per-JV dedicated space

If a JV partner contractually requires legal isolation, a different billing structure, or wants their own admin team — promote them to a dedicated space. Same content model, pushed via Contentful Migration scripts.

  • Decided per JV, not as the default
  • Maintained model parity via CI/CD migration scripts
  • Higher operational cost — only worth it for hard requirements
  • Promotion path: stay tag-scoped first, split only when needed

Shared vs scoped

What ties them together, what doesn't

Shared across all merchants

Platform
  • The product itself — one SKU, one set of attributes, one set of submission specs, one configurator schema. Marie and Lisa edit PDPs for the same Folding Box.
  • The taxonomy — both merchants categorise products the same way. Categories live in the Platform Catalog.
  • The catalog imagery — default spec shots from the supplier. Used as fallback unless the merchant overrides them.
  • The content model — same fields, same locales, same content types across every merchant.

Scoped per merchant

Merchant + CF
  • The Commercial Content — Title override, Description, USP, FAQ, Highlight Labels. Each merchant writes their own.
  • The activation state — each merchant's Merchant Catalog independently controls whether the product is sold by them.
  • The cache invalidation — when Marie publishes, Lisa's storefronts don't blink.
  • The search rules — each merchant tunes their own Algolia behaviour, search boost, filter settings.
  • The locale visibility — not every merchant sells in every market.
A small but important detail

Locales become pure language codes

In the new model, locales are just BCP-47 language identifiers — nl, nl-BE, en-GB, fr, de, es, it. They are no longer used to carry merchant identity.

The custom brand-encoded locales all retire

Today's brand-suffixed locales — nl-AGNL, nl-AGBE, de-AGDE, nl-DWM, en-brandon, en-WLS and friends — all go away. Their job (carrying merchant identity) is now done by the merchant:* tag. When Lisa works on an Allgifts product for Belgium, she's editing the nl-BE locale of an entry tagged merchant:allgifts. Two clean axes instead of one overloaded string.

Summary

One space, ten merchants, no chaos

The whole thing in one sentence

Helloprint, Allgifts, Drukwerkmax, Brandon, Quinns and every WLS shop share one Contentful space, one content model, and one set of integrations — but through tag-based role policies, each editor only ever sees their own merchant's content, while the Platform Catalog underpins all of them with shared catalog facts. Onboarding a new merchant takes a tag and a role. Promoting a JV to their own dedicated space stays available as an escape hatch when isolation becomes a hard requirement.
